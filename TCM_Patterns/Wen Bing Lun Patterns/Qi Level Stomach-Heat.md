## üîç Clinical Manifestations

### Cardinal Symptoms (CAM)
**Essential Symptoms:**
- High fever that is worse in the afternoon
- No aversion to cold
- Feeling of heat
- Intense thirst
- Profuse sweating

### Complete Symptom Picture

**Chief Symptoms:**
- High fever, often spiking in the afternoon
- Intense thirst with a desire for cold drinks
- Profuse sweating, particularly on the head and face

**Accompanying Symptoms:**
- Feeling of internal heat or burning sensation in the Stomach
- Dry mouth and throat
- Constipation with dry stools
- Restlessness and irritability
- Possible epigastric pain or discomfort
- Nausea or vomiting may occur in severe cases

**Tongue:** `Red tongue with yellow coating, potentially dry`

**Pulse:** `Overflowing-Rapid pulse`

##  Contraindications & Cautions

**Treatment Contraindications:**
- Prolonged use of cold and bitter herbs which can damage Spleen Qi
- Aggressive cooling in Spleen deficient individuals
- Use of warming herbs

**Cautions:**
- Monitor the patient for signs of Spleen Qi deficiency due to the cooling nature of the treatment.
- Adjust the dosage of herbs based on the patient's constitution and response.

**When to Avoid:**
- Avoid strong heat-clearing herbs in patients with underlying Spleen Qi deficiency, as it can weaken the digestive system.

**Biomedical Understanding:**
In Western medicine, these conditions involve inflammation and irritation of the stomach lining, often due to infection, medication, or lifestyle factors.

TCM pattern diagnosis helps differentiate the underlying imbalances contributing to these conditions, allowing for a more targeted and holistic treatment approach. For example, even with a diagnosis of gastritis, TCM can distinguish between patterns of Stomach-Heat, Food Stagnation, or Spleen Deficiency, each requiring a different treatment strategy.


## ‚úÖ Clinical Pearls & Experience

### Clinical Tips
- Always differentiate between true heat and false heat, especially in patients with Yin deficiency.
- Consider the patient's emotional state, as emotional stress can significantly contribute to Stomach-Heat.
- Educate patients on dietary modifications to avoid heat-generating foods.

### Common Mistakes
- Overlooking the role of emotions in the development of Stomach-Heat.
- Prescribing cooling herbs without considering the patient's underlying Spleen Qi.
- Failing to address underlying Yin deficiency, which can exacerbate the condition.

### Treatment Modifications
- **If Spleen Qi is deficient:** add herbs that tonify the Spleen, such as Bai Zhu (Atractylodis Macrocephalae Rhizoma).
- **If Yin is deficient:** add herbs that nourish Yin, such as Mai Men Dong (Ophiopogonis Radix).

### Success Indicators
- Reduction in fever and thirst.
- Improvement in tongue coating.
- Resolution of epigastric discomfort.
