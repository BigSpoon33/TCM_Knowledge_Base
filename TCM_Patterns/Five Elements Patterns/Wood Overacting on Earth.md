**Essential Symptoms:**
- Hypochondriac and epigastric pain

- Irritability

## Contraindications & Cautions

**Treatment Contraindications:**
- Aggressive Liver-draining formulas in Spleen-deficient patients
- Excessive cold or raw foods
- Prolonged fasting or skipping meals

**Cautions:**
- Avoid prolonged use of strongly draining herbs if the Spleen is severely deficient.
- Monitor the patient's digestive function closely during treatment.

**When to Avoid:**
- Avoid acupuncture and herbs that strongly move Qi in pregnant women with a history of miscarriage.

## Clinical Pearls & Experience

### Clinical Tips
- Always inquire about the patient's emotional state and lifestyle to identify potential causes of Liver Qi stagnation.
- Examine the tongue coating carefully to assess the presence of dampness.
- Palpate the abdomen for areas of tenderness or distension.

### Common Mistakes
- Focusing solely on Spleen tonification without addressing the underlying Liver Qi stagnation.
- Using overly draining herbs that further weaken the Spleen.

### Treatment Modifications
- **If Dampness is prominent:** Add herbs such as Fu Ling and Cang Zhu to dry dampness.
- **If Blood Deficiency is present:** Incorporate Blood-nourishing herbs such as Dang Gui and He Shou Wu.

### Success Indicators
- Improved appetite and digestion.
- Reduced abdominal distension.
- Increased energy levels.
- Improved emotional stability.
