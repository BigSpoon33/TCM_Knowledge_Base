## üõë Contraindications & Cautions

**Treatment Contraindications:**
- Excessive sweating
- Severe Qi and Blood deficiency

**Cautions:**
- Use warming herbs with caution in patients with underlying heat conditions.
- Avoid strong sweating methods in patients who are weak or deficient.

**When to Avoid:**
- Contraindicated situation: In cases of true [[Internal Wind]], addressing the root cause is crucial, rather than solely focusing on expelling external wind.


## üß™ Western Medical Correlates

**Common Western Diagnoses:**
- Common cold
- Influenza
- Allergic rhinitis
- Bell's Palsy
- Arthritis
- Urticaria

**Biomedical Understanding:**
Western medicine attributes these conditions to viral or bacterial infections, allergic reactions, or autoimmune processes. Symptoms like fever, nasal congestion, and inflammation are seen as the body's response to these triggers.

**Integration Notes:**
TCM pattern diagnosis adds to Western diagnosis by identifying the specific imbalances in the body's Qi, Blood, and Yin/Yang, which can guide treatment strategies beyond merely addressing the pathogen or allergic trigger.


## üí° Clinical Pearls & Experience

### Clinical Tips
- Always consider the season and environment when diagnosing Wind invasion.
- Ask about sudden onset of symptoms and shifting pain to help confirm the diagnosis.
- Palpate the pulse carefully to determine the presence and strength of floating pulse.

### Common Mistakes
- Mistaking aversion to cold in [[Wind-Cold]] for aversion to wind in plain Wind.
- Ignoring underlying deficiencies, which can make the body more vulnerable to wind invasion.

### Treatment Modifications
- If sweating is profuse, reduce the dosage of warming, dispersing herbs.
- If there is underlying Qi Deficiency, combine wind-expelling herbs with Qi tonics.

### Success Indicators
- Reduction in aversion to wind.
- Alleviation of headache and body aches.
