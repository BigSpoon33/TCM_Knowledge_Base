# {{TOPIC_NAME}}

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), Jueyin Headache (厥陰頭痛) refers to a specific headache pattern characterized by pain predominantly located at the vertex, or top, of the head. This type of headache is rooted in the complexities of the Jueyin stage, the deepest level of the six stages described in the classic text *Shang Han Lun* (傷寒論, Treatise on Cold Damage). Understanding Jueyin Headache requires considering its significance, typical presentation, and historical context within TCM theory.

**Significance of Jueyin Headache:**

Jueyin Headache is significant as it often indicates a deep-seated constitutional imbalance. It reflects a disruption in the harmonious flow of Qi (vital energy) and Blood, particularly affecting the Liver and its associated meridian pathways. The Jueyin stage represents a complex interplay of heat and cold, deficiency and excess within the body. Therefore, Jueyin Headache suggests a more intricate and often chronic condition compared to headaches affecting more superficial meridians. Addressing the underlying Jueyin imbalance is crucial for effective and lasting relief.

**Typical Presentation:**

The hallmark of Jueyin Headache is its location at the vertex of the head. However, it's important to note the associated symptoms, aggravating factors, and tongue/pulse diagnosis to accurately differentiate this pattern from other headache types in TCM.

**Clinical Manifestations:**

*   **Location:** Vertex (top) of the head is the primary location of pain.

*   **Nature of Pain:**
    *   **Throbbing:** A pounding or pulsing sensation, often associated with Liver Yang Rising. *Example: Headache worsens with anger and feels like a throbbing sensation at the top of the head.*
    *   **Stabbing:** A sharp, piercing pain that may indicate Blood Stasis. *Example: Sudden, intense stabbing pain at the vertex, especially after an injury.*
    *   **Dull/Aching:** A persistent, less intense pain, potentially suggesting Blood Deficiency or Phlegm accumulation. *Example: Chronic, dull ache at the top of the head that is worse with fatigue.*

*   **Associated Symptoms:**
    *   **Nausea:** Frequent, particularly when Liver Qi Stagnation or Phlegm is involved. *Example: Headache accompanied by a feeling of nausea and a lack of appetite.*
    *   **Vomiting:** Indicates disharmony affecting the Stomach and intestines. *Example: Severe headache that leads to vomiting.*
    *   **Cold Limbs:** Suggests deficiency of Yang Qi or blockage preventing Qi and Blood from reaching the extremities. *Example: Headache along with cold hands and feet, even in warm weather.*
    *   **Visual Disturbances:** Blurred vision, floaters, or sensitivity to light, which can point to Liver Blood Deficiency or Liver Yang Rising. *Example: Headache accompanied by blurred vision or seeing floaters.*
    *   **Dizziness/Vertigo:** Common with Liver Yang Rising or Phlegm Dampness. *Example: Headache with a sensation of spinning or lightheadedness.*
    *   **Irritability:** Often seen with Liver Qi Stagnation. *Example: Headache accompanied by increased frustration and a short temper.*

*   **Aggravating Factors:**
    *   **Stress:** Worsens Liver Qi Stagnation. *Example: Headache is significantly worse during periods of high stress at work.*
    *   **Emotional Upset:** Similar to stress. *Example: Headache triggered by arguments or emotional distress.*
    *   **Fatigue:** Exacerbates Blood Deficiency. *Example: Headache is much worse after a long day of physical activity.*
    *   **Weather Changes (windy, cold):** Can trigger headaches due to external invasion. *Example: Headache starts when exposed to cold wind.*
    *   **Diet (fatty, greasy foods):** Aggravates Phlegm Dampness. *Example: Headache is worse after eating a heavy, greasy meal.*
    *   **Menstruation (in women):** Can exacerbate Blood Deficiency. *Example: Headache consistently occurs or worsens during the menstrual cycle.*

*   **Alleviating Factors:**
    *   **Rest:** Can alleviate headaches due to Blood Deficiency or Fatigue. *Example: Headache improves after taking a nap.*
    *   **Warm Compress:** May help with headaches due to Cold or Blood Stasis. *Example: Applying a warm compress to the head provides some relief.*
    *   **Acupressure:** Can help relieve Liver Qi Stagnation and tension. *Example: Applying pressure to acupressure points on the Liver meridian provides some relief.*

*   **Tongue Diagnosis:**
    *   **Pale:** Suggests Blood Deficiency. *Example: Pale tongue body.*
    *   **Red:** Indicates Heat (e.g., Liver Yang Rising, Phlegm Fire). *Example: Red tongue body, especially on the sides.*
    *   **Purple:** Indicates Blood Stasis. *Example: Purple tinge to the tongue body, or purple spots.*
    *   **Greasy Coating:** Suggests Phlegm Dampness. *Example: Thick, greasy coating on the tongue.*
    *   **Thin, White Coating:** Suggests Deficiency. *Example: Thin coating or absence of coating on the tongue.*

*   **Pulse Diagnosis:**
    *   **Wiry:** Common with Liver Qi Stagnation or Liver Yang Rising.
    *   **Thin/Weak:** Suggests Blood Deficiency.
    *   **Choppy:** Indicates Blood Stasis.

**Historical Context:**

While the term "Jueyin Headache" isn't explicitly defined as a distinct diagnosis in the *Shang Han Lun*, the text lays the foundation for understanding this headache type through its discussion of Jueyin stage symptoms and treatment principles. The *Shang Han Lun* describes symptoms like vomiting, cold limbs, and alternating sensations of heat and cold, which can manifest as headaches within the Jueyin pattern. Later TCM practitioners have built upon these principles to more specifically define and treat Jueyin Headache. The concept of Liver Wind, developed in later dynasties, is also closely linked to Jueyin patterns and headache presentations. Therefore, a deep understanding of the *Shang Han Lun* and subsequent TCM texts is essential for effectively addressing Jueyin Headache.

## Overview

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), Jueyin Headache (厥陰頭痛) refers to a specific type of headache characterized by pain primarily located at the vertex, or the top of the head. This condition is understood within the framework of the six stages described in the *Shang Han Lun* (傷寒論, Treatise on Cold Damage), with the Jueyin stage representing the deepest and most internal level. This signifies a complex pathological state often involving a combination of cold and heat, deficiency and excess, pointing to a fundamental imbalance within the body. Due to its depth, Jueyin headaches frequently involve intricate patterns related to the Liver and Blood.

Jueyin Headache is significant because it often reflects a deeper, more chronic constitutional imbalance, rather than a superficial ailment. The pain location indicates a disruption in the flow of Qi and Blood, particularly within the Liver meridian and its associated functions. Understanding Jueyin Headache allows TCM practitioners to identify and address the root cause of the condition, offering targeted treatments that address the underlying imbalance. Recognizing this type of headache helps differentiate it from headaches stemming from other meridian involvements, such as Taiyang or Shaoyang headaches, which have different clinical presentations and treatment strategies. This distinction is vital for accurate diagnosis and effective therapeutic intervention.

While the *Shang Han Lun* doesn't explicitly use the term "Jueyin Headache" as a distinct diagnosis, it describes clinical features associated with the Jueyin stage that are directly relevant to this type of headache. These features, such as vomiting, cold limbs, and alternating sensations of heat and cold, provide a historical and theoretical basis for understanding Jueyin Headache. Later TCM literature and practitioners built upon this foundation, further defining and refining the diagnostic and treatment principles. The concept of Liver Wind, discussed in later dynasties, is also closely linked to the manifestation of Jueyin Headache. Therefore, understanding the historical context and the Jueyin stage in TCM is crucial for effectively managing this specific headache type.

## Clinical Manifestations

**Clinical Manifestations of Jueyin Headache in TCM**

Jueyin Headache, rooted in the deepest level of the six stages, presents a complex interplay of symptoms reflecting the internal imbalance, primarily involving the Liver and Blood. The hallmark is its location at the vertex of the head.

*   **Cardinal Symptoms:**
    *   **Location:** Pain specifically localized at the vertex (top) of the head.

*   **Nature of Pain:**
    *   **Throbbing:** A pounding or pulsing sensation at the vertex, often associated with Liver Yang Rising.
    *   **Dull, Aching:** A persistent, less intense pain, suggesting Blood Deficiency or Phlegm accumulation.
    *   **Stabbing:** Sharp, piercing pain, indicative of Blood Stasis.

*   **Accompanying Symptoms:**

    *   **Digestive:**
        *   Nausea: Frequently present, especially with Liver Qi Stagnation affecting the Stomach.
        *   Vomiting: Similar to nausea, indicates disharmony between the Liver, Stomach, and Intestines.

    *   **Extremities:**
        *   Cold Limbs: A common indicator of Yang Qi Deficiency or impaired circulation of Qi and Blood.

    *   **Neurological/Sensory:**
        *   Dizziness/Vertigo: Lightheadedness or a spinning sensation, often linked to Liver Yang Rising or Phlegm Dampness obstructing the head.
        *   Visual Disturbances: Blurred vision, floaters, or sensitivity to light, suggesting Liver Blood Deficiency or Liver Yang Rising affecting the eyes.

    *   **Emotional/Psychological:**
        *   Irritability: Proneness to frustration and anger, characteristic of Liver Qi Stagnation.

*   **Aggravating Factors:**

    *   **Emotional:** Stress and emotional upset exacerbate Liver Qi Stagnation, triggering or worsening the headache.
    *   **Physical:** Fatigue can deplete Blood and Qi, making the headache more pronounced.

    *   **Dietary:** Consumption of fatty or greasy foods can lead to Phlegm Dampness, aggravating the headache.
    *   **Hormonal (in women):** Menstruation can exacerbate Blood Deficiency, making headaches more frequent or intense.

*   **Alleviating Factors:**

    *   Rest: Can improve headaches related to Blood Deficiency or fatigue.
    *   Warm Compress: Applying warmth can ease headaches resulting from Cold or Blood Stasis.
    *   Acupressure: Stimulating specific acupoints can relieve Liver Qi Stagnation and associated tension.

*   **Tongue Diagnosis:**

    *   **Color:**
        *   Pale Tongue: Suggests underlying Blood Deficiency.
        *   Red Tongue: Indicates Heat, such as Liver Yang Rising or Phlegm Fire.
        *   Purple Tongue: Points to Blood Stasis.
    *   **Coating:**
        *   Greasy Coating: Suggests Phlegm Dampness accumulation.
        *   Thin, White Coating: May indicate a Deficiency condition.

*   **Pulse Diagnosis:**

    *   Wiry Pulse: A tense, string-like pulse commonly associated with Liver Qi Stagnation or Liver Yang Rising.
    *   Thin/Weak Pulse: Indicates Blood Deficiency.
    *   Choppy Pulse: Suggests Blood Stasis.

## Etiology & Pathomechanisms

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), Jueyin Headache etiology and pathomechanisms are complex, reflecting the Jueyin stage's nature as the deepest level of illness. The root causes are typically internal imbalances rather than direct external pathogenic invasions, though external factors can exacerbate pre-existing conditions.

**Primary Etiological Factors:**

*   **Liver Qi Stagnation:** Emotional stress, frustration, and repressed anger impair the Liver's function of ensuring the smooth flow of Qi. This stagnation can lead to Qi rising upwards, manifesting as a vertex headache.
*   **Liver Blood Deficiency:** Insufficient Liver Blood fails to nourish the head, resulting in a dull, aching headache, often accompanied by dizziness, blurred vision, and fatigue. Blood deficiency can arise from chronic illness, poor diet, or excessive blood loss (e.g., heavy menstruation).
*   **Liver Yang Rising:** Prolonged Liver Qi Stagnation can transform into Heat, leading to Liver Yang rising upwards. This manifests as a throbbing headache at the vertex, often accompanied by irritability, red face, and a wiry pulse. This may be due to Kidney Yin Deficiency failing to anchor Liver Yang.
*   **Blood Stasis:** Impaired Qi and Blood circulation, often resulting from trauma, prolonged stagnation, or underlying Blood Deficiency, can lead to localized Blood Stasis in the head. This manifests as a stabbing or fixed pain, often worse at night.
*   **Phlegm Dampness:** Improper diet (rich, greasy, or cold foods), weakened Spleen function, or chronic fluid retention can lead to Phlegm accumulation. When Phlegm rises upwards, it obstructs the clear Yang Qi, causing a dull, heavy headache, often accompanied by nausea and a greasy tongue coating.
*   **Kidney Deficiency:** Kidney Jing and Yin deficiency can fail to nourish the Liver (Water not nourishing Wood), indirectly leading to Liver imbalances and headache.

**Pathomechanisms:**

The development of Jueyin Headache involves a complex interplay of disrupted Qi and Blood flow, organ disharmony, and the generation of internal pathogens.

1.  **Initial Imbalance:** The process usually starts with one or more of the primary etiological factors, such as Liver Qi Stagnation.

2.  **Meridian Obstruction:** The stagnation or deficiency then affects the meridians, particularly the Liver meridian (which traverses the vertex) and the Du meridian (Governing Vessel), which also passes through the vertex. This obstruction prevents the normal flow of Qi and Blood to the head.

3.  **Upward Disturbance:** The impaired flow of Qi and Blood, or the rising of Liver Yang or Phlegm, disrupts the clear Yang Qi in the head, causing pain and other associated symptoms.

4.  **Visceral Disharmony:** The headache is rarely an isolated symptom. The disharmony often extends to other Zang-Fu organs, particularly the Liver, Spleen, and Kidneys, leading to a constellation of symptoms beyond just head pain.

**Disease Progression and Common Transformations:**

Jueyin Headache often starts as an acute or episodic condition but can become chronic if the underlying imbalances are not addressed. Common transformations include:

*   **Qi Stagnation Transforming to Heat:** Untreated Liver Qi Stagnation can generate Heat, leading to Liver Yang Rising headache with a throbbing nature.
*   **Blood Deficiency Leading to Stasis:** Chronic Blood Deficiency can impair circulation, resulting in Blood Stasis and a more fixed, stabbing pain.
*   **Phlegm Dampness Obstructing Yang Qi:** Over time, Phlegm Dampness can increasingly obstruct the clear Yang Qi in the head, leading to chronic, dull headaches with a heavy sensation.
*   **Kidney Deficiency Exacerbating Liver Imbalance:** As Kidney Jing or Yin depletes with age or chronic illness, the Liver's ability to be nourished is compromised, worsening Liver-related headaches.

**Example:** A person experiencing chronic stress (Liver Qi Stagnation) might initially present with a headache that worsens with stress. Over time, the Qi Stagnation can lead to Liver Yang Rising, transforming the headache into a throbbing pain with irritability. If the condition persists, Liver Blood Deficiency may develop, resulting in dizziness and blurred vision alongside the headache.

## Differential Diagnosis

When diagnosing Jueyin Headache in TCM, it's crucial to differentiate it from other headache patterns that may present with overlapping symptoms. The key lies in carefully assessing the location, nature of the pain, accompanying symptoms, tongue and pulse findings, and aggravating/alleviating factors to pinpoint the underlying imbalance. Here's a breakdown of how Jueyin Headache compares to other common headache types in TCM:

**1. Liver Yang Rising Headache:**

This is perhaps the most common point of confusion. Both Jueyin Headache and Liver Yang Rising headache can involve the vertex and throbbing pain.

| Feature            | Jueyin Headache                                                                    | Liver Yang Rising Headache                                                              |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Location**       | Vertex (top of head), often deep.                                                  | Vertex, temples, or entire head.                                                       |
| **Nature of Pain**  | Throbbing, dull, stabbing; variable and can be complex.                           | Primarily throbbing and distending, often described as a "rising" sensation.            |
| **Associated Symptoms** | Nausea, vomiting, cold limbs, emotional lability, fatigue, visual disturbances.     | Irritability, dizziness, tinnitus, red face, insomnia, blurred vision.                 |
| **Tongue**           | Variable: Pale, red, purple; greasy or thin coating. Reflects the underlying pattern. | Red body, possibly with a yellow coating, often redder on the sides.                |
| **Pulse**            | Wiry, thin, choppy; again, dependent on the specific pattern.                       | Wiry, forceful, rapid.                                                                |
| **Key Distinguishing Feature** | Presence of cold limbs and a more complex presentation indicative of a deeper imbalance. | Clear signs of excess Heat, such as red face and forceful pulse.                         |
| **TCM Pattern**       | Complex patterns involving Liver Blood Deficiency, Liver Qi Stagnation, Phlegm, and internal Cold/Heat.    | Liver Yang Rising due to Liver Yin Deficiency.                                          |

*   **Decision Point:** If the primary presentation is throbbing pain, irritability, a red face, and a forceful pulse, Liver Yang Rising is more likely. If cold limbs, nausea/vomiting, and a more variable presentation are present, consider Jueyin Headache.

**2. Taiyang Headache:**

Taiyang headaches involve the occipital region and/or the back of the neck, following the Taiyang meridian.

| Feature            | Jueyin Headache                                                    | Taiyang Headache                                          |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| **Location**       | Vertex (top of head).                                             | Occipital region, back of neck, possibly whole head. |
| **Nature of Pain**  | Variable; throbbing, dull, stabbing.                             | Stiff, achy, tight.                                      |
| **Associated Symptoms** | Nausea, vomiting, cold limbs (may be present).               | Stiff neck, aversion to cold, possibly fever.            |
| **Key Distinguishing Feature** | Vertex location, internal signs.                             | Occipital location, external invasion signs.              |

*   **Decision Point:** Location is the primary differentiating factor.  Taiyang headaches are often related to external Wind-Cold invasion.

**3. Shaoyang Headache:**

Shaoyang headaches typically affect the sides of the head and temples.

| Feature            | Jueyin Headache                                                    | Shaoyang Headache                                         |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| **Location**       | Vertex (top of head).                                             | Temples, sides of the head.                             |
| **Nature of Pain**  | Variable.                                                           | Alternating pain intensity, fullness sensation.             |
| **Associated Symptoms** | Nausea, vomiting, cold limbs (may be present).               | Bitter taste in mouth, dry throat, alternating chills and fever. |
| **Key Distinguishing Feature** | Vertex location, internal signs.                             | Temporal location, alternating symptoms.                 |

*   **Decision Point:** Location is again a key factor. Shaoyang headaches often suggest disharmony between the Liver and Gallbladder.

**4. Blood Deficiency Headache:**

Blood Deficiency headaches often present as a dull ache that worsens with fatigue.

| Feature            | Jueyin Headache                                                                       | Blood Deficiency Headache                                                                      |
|---------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Location**       | Vertex (top of head).                                                                | Can be anywhere, often diffuse.                                                              |
| **Nature of Pain**  | Variable; can be dull, throbbing, or stabbing, depending on the underlying pattern.  | Dull, aching, often feels "empty" or "hollow".                                                |
| **Associated Symptoms** | Nausea, vomiting, cold limbs, emotional lability.                                 | Dizziness, fatigue, pale complexion, blurred vision, poor memory, insomnia.                    |
| **Tongue**           | Variable, depending on the pattern; may be pale.                                     | Pale.                                                                                        |
| **Pulse**            | Variable; may be thin.                                                              | Thin, weak.                                                                                  |
| **Key Distinguishing Feature** | Presence of other Jueyin signs (cold limbs, nausea/vomiting, complex presentation). | Primarily characterized by signs of Blood Deficiency.                                         |

*   **Decision Point:** Determine if the headache is part of a larger picture of Blood Deficiency. A pale tongue, thin pulse, and symptoms like dizziness and fatigue strongly suggest Blood Deficiency.  While Blood Deficiency can be a *component* of a Jueyin Headache, it is not the defining feature.

**5. Phlegm-Dampness Headache:**

Phlegm-Dampness headaches often feel heavy and dull.

| Feature            | Jueyin Headache                                                                 | Phlegm-Dampness Headache                                                               |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Location**       | Vertex (top of head).                                                          | Can be anywhere, often feels heavy in the head.                                       |
| **Nature of Pain**  | Variable; can be dull or throbbing.                                             | Dull, heavy, and often accompanied by a feeling of pressure.                              |
| **Associated Symptoms** | Nausea, vomiting, cold limbs (may be present).                            | Heaviness in the limbs, chest oppression, poor appetite, digestive issues.            |
| **Tongue**           | Variable, depending on the pattern; may have a greasy coating.                    | Swollen with a greasy coating.                                                         |
| **Pulse**            | Slippery, wiry                                                                | Slippery.                                                                               |
| **Key Distinguishing Feature** | Vertex location, internal signs.                                             | Heaviness, digestive symptoms, greasy tongue coating.                                    |

*   **Decision Point:** Look for signs of Dampness. Heaviness, digestive issues, and a greasy tongue point towards Phlegm-Dampness.

**In Summary:**

Differentiating Jueyin Headache requires a holistic assessment, considering the specific location of the vertex, the variable nature of the pain, the presence of accompanying symptoms such as cold limbs and nausea, and careful observation of the tongue and pulse. By systematically comparing these features with other headache patterns, a practitioner can arrive at a more accurate diagnosis and develop an appropriate treatment strategy. The complexity of Jueyin Headache highlights the importance of understanding the deeper constitutional imbalances reflected in the Jueyin stage.

The treatment of Jueyin Headache in TCM relies heavily on herbal formulas tailored to the specific underlying pattern. Given the complexity of Jueyin patterns, accurate diagnosis is crucial. These formulas address the root cause of the headache, aiming to restore balance within the body. Here's an overview of common patterns and corresponding herbal formulas:

**1. Liver Qi Stagnation:**

*   **Pattern Presentation:** Vertex headache often described as distending or throbbing, aggravated by stress and emotional upset. Other symptoms include irritability, sighing, chest or rib-side discomfort, and a wiry pulse.
*   **Underlying Mechanism:** Stagnant Liver Qi disrupts the smooth flow of Qi and Blood, leading to pain.

## Clinical Applications

**Clinical Applications of Jueyin Headache in TCM**

Jueyin Headache, characterized by pain at the vertex (top) of the head, represents a complex TCM pattern reflecting imbalances at a deep, constitutional level. Its diagnosis and treatment require careful differentiation based on the specific presenting symptoms and underlying patterns.

*   **Pale:** Suggests Blood Deficiency.
*   **Red:** Indicates Heat, potentially Liver Yang Rising or Phlegm Fire.
*   **Purple:** Points towards Blood Stasis.
*   **Greasy Coating:** Suggests Phlegm Dampness.
*   **Thin, White Coating:** Suggests Deficiency.

**Pulse Presentation:** The pulse further refines the diagnosis.

*   **Wiry:** Common in Liver Qi Stagnation or Liver Yang Rising.
*   **Thin/Weak:** Suggests Blood Deficiency.
*   **Choppy:** Indicates Blood Stasis.

**Accompanying Symptoms and Pattern Differentiation:** Beyond the cardinal symptom of vertex headache, the presence of other signs and symptoms guides pattern differentiation, leading to tailored treatment strategies. Key patterns and associated symptoms include:

*   **Liver Qi Stagnation:**
    *   **Nature of Pain:** Distending, throbbing.
    *   **Accompanying Symptoms:** Irritability, depression, sighing, abdominal distension, premenstrual tension (in women).
    *   **Aggravating Factors:** Stress, emotional upset.
    *   **Treatment Principle:** Soothe the Liver, regulate Qi, alleviate pain.

*   **Liver Blood Deficiency:**
    *   **Nature of Pain:** Dull, aching, may be better with rest.
    *   **Accompanying Symptoms:** Dizziness, blurred vision, floaters, fatigue, pale complexion, insomnia, scanty menstruation (in women).

*   **Liver Yang Rising:**
    *   **Nature of Pain:** Throbbing, often intense, can feel like head is about to explode.
    *   **Accompanying Symptoms:** Dizziness, tinnitus, red face, irritability, insomnia, bitter taste in the mouth.
    *   **Aggravating Factors:** Stress, anger.
    *   **Treatment Principle:** Subdue Liver Yang, nourish Kidney Yin, alleviate pain.

*   **Blood Stasis:**
    *   **Nature of Pain:** Stabbing, fixed, localized.
    *   **Accompanying Symptoms:** History of trauma, dark complexion, purple lips, fixed masses.
    *   **Treatment Principle:** Invigorate Blood, dispel Blood Stasis, alleviate pain.

*   **Phlegm Dampness:**
    *   **Nature of Pain:** Dull, heavy, oppressive.
    *   **Accompanying Symptoms:** Nausea, vomiting, dizziness, heaviness in the head, poor appetite, digestive issues, edema.
    *   **Aggravating Factors:** Fatty foods, damp weather.
    *   **Treatment Principle:** Transform Phlegm, resolve Dampness, strengthen the Spleen, alleviate pain.

*   **Wind Invasion (External):**
    *   **Nature of Pain:** May be accompanied by aversion to wind or cold.
    *   **Accompanying Symptoms:** Stiff neck, fever, body aches, runny nose.
    *   **Aggravating Factors:** Exposure to wind or cold.
    *   **Treatment Principle:** Expel wind, resolve exterior, alleviate pain.
