## 3. Etiology & Pathomechanisms

**Question:**
What causes Etiology & Pathomechanisms and how does it develop?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
**Primary Etiological Factors:**
*   **Spleen Qi Deficiency (脾气虚):** The Spleen governs transformation and transportation of fluids. When Spleen Qi is deficient, it fails to properly transform fluids, leading to their accumulation. This is a very common cause of edema in TCM.
*   **Kidney Yang Deficiency (肾阳虚):** The Kidney governs water metabolism and provides the "fire" needed to transform fluids. Kidney Yang deficiency results in a failure to warm and transform fluids, leading to edema, especially in the lower body. It also impacts the Kidney's ability to control opening and closing (urination).
*   **Lung Qi Deficiency (肺气虚):** The Lung governs Qi and regulates the water passages. Lung Qi deficiency impairs the Lung's ability to descend and disperse fluids, leading to fluid accumulation, often manifesting as facial or upper body edema.
*   **External Pathogenic Factors (外邪):** Invasion of Wind, Cold, Dampness, or Heat can disrupt the body's fluid metabolism.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

**Question:**
How would you distinguish Differential Diagnosis from similar patterns?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
**Similar Conditions & Patterns to Consider:**
*   **Dampness (湿):** While edema (*Shui Zhong*) is a specific manifestation of fluid imbalance, underlying Dampness is often a root cause. Dampness can manifest without overt edema, causing symptoms like heaviness, fatigue, and a sticky coating on the tongue.
*   **Distinguishing Feature:** Edema is characterized by visible swelling, while Dampness may only present with subjective sensations of heaviness or discomfort without noticeable swelling.
*   **Decision Point:** Is there visible swelling, especially pitting edema? If yes, edema is more likely. If not, consider Dampness alone.
*   **Phlegm (痰):** In TCM, Phlegm is a thicker, more congealed form of Dampness. While Phlegm can contribute to edema, it also manifests as nodules, masses, or mental cloudiness.

## 5. Treatment Principles

**Question:**
What are the main treatment principles for Treatment Principles?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
**Primary Treatment Principles:**
*   **Strengthen the Spleen (健脾 - Jian Pi):** Fortify the Spleen's ability to transform and transport fluids. This is crucial for most edema patterns.
*   **Warm and Tonify the Kidney Yang (温补肾阳 - Wen Bu Shen Yang):** Enhance the Kidney's function in governing water metabolism and Qi transformation, particularly for lower body edema and cold patterns.
*   **Promote Lung Qi Circulation (宣肺利水 - Xuan Fei Li Shui):** Facilitate the Lung's role in dispersing and downbearing fluids, useful for edema affecting the upper body and face.
*   **Resolve Dampness (祛湿 - Qu Shi):** Eliminate excess dampness that obstructs fluid circulation.

## 7. Acupuncture Points

**Question:**
Which acupuncture points would you use for Acupuncture Points?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
**I. Points for Spleen Qi Deficiency:**
*   **SP 3 (Taibai):** The Shu-Stream and Yuan-Source point of the Spleen channel. It strengthens the Spleen, resolves dampness, and promotes digestion. Excellent for edema associated with fatigue, poor appetite, and loose stools.
*   **SP 6 (Sanyinjiao):** The intersection point of the Spleen, Liver, and Kidney channels. Tonifies the Spleen, transforms dampness, and harmonizes the lower burner. It is a vital point for almost all edema patterns but especially useful when Kidney involvement is suspected. *Caution: Contraindicated in pregnancy.*
*   **SP 9 (Yinlingquan):** The He-Sea point of the Spleen channel. Drains dampness from the lower burner. A key point for treating lower limb edema, especially when associated with a heavy sensation.
*   **ST 36 (Zusanli):** While on the Stomach channel, this is one of the most versatile points in TCM. Tonifies Qi and Blood, strengthens the Spleen and Stomach, and resolves dampness. Addresses root deficiency to improve overall fluid metabolism.
