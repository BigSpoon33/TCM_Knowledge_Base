*   **Aversion to Wind (怕風; *Pà Fēng*) or Slight Aversion to Cold (怕冷; *Pà Lěng*):** Less intense than with Wind Cold. Patients may experience a mild chill initially that quickly transitions to feeling hot.

## 3. Etiology & Pathomechanisms

**Question:**
What causes Etiology & Pathomechanisms and how does it develop?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
**Primary Causes:**
*   **Seasonal Factors:** Most prevalent during spring and summer when both Wind and Heat are abundant in the environment.
*   **Environmental Exposure:** Direct exposure to hot, windy conditions, such as being outdoors in hot weather without adequate protection.
*   **Weakened Wei Qi (Defensive Qi):** A weakened immune system makes an individual more susceptible to external pathogens. Lifestyle factors like poor diet, lack of sleep, chronic stress, and pre-existing conditions can impair Wei Qi.
*   **Crowded Environments:** Close proximity to individuals carrying the pathogen increases the risk of transmission, such as in schools or public transportation.

## 5. Treatment Principles

**Question:**
What are the main treatment principles for Treatment Principles?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
**Primary Formulas/Interventions:**
*   **Sang Ju Yin (桑菊饮):** This is a foundational formula specifically designed for External Wind Heat affecting the Lungs. It focuses on gently dispersing Wind, clearing Heat, and moistening dryness. It's particularly useful for early-stage symptoms like cough, mild fever, and headache.
*   **Yin Qiao San (银翘散):** This formula is stronger than Sang Ju Yin and is used when Heat signs are more pronounced, such as a significant sore throat, high fever, and yellow nasal discharge. It excels at detoxifying and clearing Heat from the upper Jiao.
*   **Ma Xing Shi Gan Tang (麻杏石甘汤):** While primarily used for Heat in the Lungs, this formula can be appropriate if Wind Heat has quickly transformed into Lung Heat, presenting with wheezing and rapid breathing. It strongly clears Lung Heat and descends Lung Qi.
